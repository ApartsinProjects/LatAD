Research Report · Proof of Concept

Physically-Grounded Anomaly Detection for Massively-Multimodal Cyber-Physical Normal Data

Alexander Apartsin

Holon Institute of Technology (HIT)

Preliminary synthetic-benchmark study · real-testbed validation (WADI/SWaT/HAI) in progress

Abstract

Anomaly detection in transportation cyber-physical systems (CPS) is limited less by the scarcity of faults than by the difficulty of modelling normal behaviour. CPS normal operation is Massive, Implicit, Imbalanced, Multimodal (MIIM): it comprises a very large number of unlabeled operating modes whose frequencies span orders of magnitude. We argue this structure produces three characteristic detector errors — false positives on rare-but-valid modes, false negatives on faults hidden in the interpolated gaps between modes, and an inability to calibrate or rank detectors without labeled faults — and we present a framework that turns the structure into an advantage. A joint variational latent model with a Gaussian-mixture prior (VaDE) discovers the modes; on top of it three components each target one error: (C1) physically-guided anomaly generation that manufactures controllable, physically-valid boundary faults; (C2) a basin-of-attraction test that separates rare-but-valid modes from faults by latent dynamics; and (C3) a cluster-based reconstruction ensemble that catches over-interpolated faults. On a controlled MIIM benchmark, the joint foundation reaches AUROC 0.94 versus 0.74–0.87 for standard detectors, and the full framework 0.997 (C3 being the decisive detection driver); C2 lowers the rare-mode false-positive rate from 0.93 to 0.69 in its dedicated configuration; and on physically-valid dependency-violation faults, Isolation Forest and a single autoencoder are effectively blind (TPR 0.00 at 5% FPR) while the framework detects 0.92. We report a full ablation isolating each component, including where a component is neutral or its benefit configuration-dependent, and are explicit about what is and is not yet demonstrated: results are on synthetic data; validation on the WADI water-distribution testbed is the next step.

1. Introduction

Modern transportation platforms — road vehicles, rail rolling stock, maritime vessels — are deeply integrated cyber-physical systems: networks of sensors, embedded software, control logic, and physical actuators that continuously sense their own state and act upon it. A contemporary locomotive coordinates hundreds of electronic control units and thousands of signals governing propulsion, braking, and energy management.

This complexity makes a system's faults impossible to enumerate in advance: the combinations in which a fault can arise cannot be predefined or exhaustively sampled before deployment, and faults often develop slowly as wear or drift that produces no obvious symptom until well advanced. Because faults cannot be specified ahead of time, they must be discovered from the system's own behaviour as it operates — the task of anomaly detection. That behaviour is richly observable: high-frequency vibration, current, temperature, pressure and control signals are recorded across entire fleets, and in principle carry an early signature of almost any developing fault. Detecting those signatures before they culminate in failure is of direct operational and societal importance: in transportation an undetected fault is not merely a maintenance expense but a potential safety event.

Anomaly detection is commonly framed as characterising anomalies, yet this framing is misleading. Were labeled faults abundant, the task would reduce to supervised classification; in practice genuine faults are rare and unrepresentative. The principal source of information lies on the opposite side of the decision boundary, in the normal data, which is abundant yet rarely exploited in full. Our thesis is that the hard and neglected problem is modelling normal behaviour, and that CPS normal data has a specific, exploitable structure we call MIIM (Section 3). We identify three detector errors this structure induces and contribute a framework, not a single model, in which a joint latent-clustering foundation is augmented by three targeted components (Section 5), validated on a controlled benchmark and on physically-valid synthetic faults (Section 6).

2. Related Work

CPS/ICS anomaly-detection benchmarks and methods

Industrial control-system testbeds provide the standard evaluation ground: SWaT and WADI (water treatment and distribution) [1,2] and the HIL-based HAI dataset [3], with the simulated BATADAL water-network competition [4]. Multivariate time-series detectors evaluated on these include reconstruction/forecasting models (OmniAnomaly [5], USAD [6], TranAD [7]), graph models (GDN [8], MTAD-GAT [9]), GAN-based MAD-GAN [10], and the Anomaly Transformer [11]; Ruff et al. [12] give a unifying review of deep and shallow anomaly detection. We note that several results in this line rely on point-adjustment scoring later shown to inflate F1 [13], and we deliberately avoid it.

Deep and shallow detectors

Foundational families include reconstruction (autoencoders, the VAE [14]), one-class methods (OC-SVM, Deep SVDD [15], DROCC [16]), density/energy models (DAGMM [17]), self-supervised transformation learning (NeuTraL-AD [18]), and non-deep baselines — Isolation Forest [19], LOF [20], and the parameter-free ECOD/COPOD [21]. We use Isolation Forest, LOF, and a deep autoencoder as baselines spanning the global, local-density, and single-reconstruction families.

Deep clustering with generative latent models

Our foundation builds on latent-variable clustering: VaDE [22] replaces the VAE's single-Gaussian prior with a Gaussian mixture, jointly generative and clustering; GMVAE [23] documents the cluster-degeneracy failure we encounter and fix; DAGMM [17] is the anomaly-focused member of this family. Training instability and collapse are known [23], with fixes including KL/β-annealing and mini-batch mixture initialisation (S3VDC [24]).

Multimodal normal, synthetic anomalies, and whitening

That "normal" is multimodal is well known in prognostics — C-MAPSS [25] contains six operating regimes — and contextual anomaly detection [26] formalises why ignoring regime causes false positives; yet no established CPS method isolates the rare-but-valid-mode false positive that we target, a genuine gap. For synthetic anomalies, outlier exposure [27] and CutPaste [28] create proxy irregularities; channel-shuffling to destroy cross-sensor joint structure (our C1) has no established named predecessor and is presented as a contribution. Label-free detector ranking has been studied via excess-mass/mass-volume curves [29]. Our whitened residual score uses Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage covariance [30] and follows the Mahalanobis-distance OOD line [31]. The operational cost of false alarms in rail predictive maintenance is documented in CBM/PdM surveys [32,33].

3. Problem Setting and Assumptions

Let normal operation be a distribution p(x) over sensor windows x∈R^d. Our central assumption is that for CPS, p(x) is a mixture over many operating modes with four properties we jointly term MIIM:

  • Multimodal — many separate but equally valid operating modes, not one.
  • Massive — the number of modes is very large.
  • Implicit — modes are neither labeled nor declared; they must be discovered.
  • Imbalanced — mode frequencies span orders of magnitude; the rarest legitimate modes are represented by only a handful of examples.

CPS normal data differs from the data anomaly-detection methods usually assume: it is generated by deterministic physics, hard physical limits, and engineered control, not a single stochastic process. This makes valid states combinatorially numerous (the origin of massive multimodality) but also endows them with genuine structure a physically-informed detector can exploit.

Three characteristic errors. MIIM induces three detector failures, distinct from any fault of the vehicle itself. (i) The false positive: a rare but legitimate mode, seen too seldom in training, falls outside the learned normal model and is alarmed on — eroding operator trust. (ii) The false negative: between two well-sampled modes a single model interpolates a smooth bridge of "normal" the system never visits, and a genuine fault whose signature falls in that pocket is scored as ordinary. (iii) Calibration and ranking: because representative abnormal data does not exist, thresholds cannot be fixed and competing detectors cannot be ranked. The framework of Section 5 targets these three errors with three components.

4. Data Construction

Absent access-cleared real data (Section 7), we build a controlled MIIM benchmark that makes the four properties explicit and provides ground-truth modes for measurement. Normal data is a mixture of K=40 operating modes; each mode is a smooth nonlinear manifold (a low-dimensional regime coordinate mapped through a mode-specific random embedding with a channel-coupling nonlinearity), mimicking "valid states must remain consistent with physics/control". Mode weights follow a Zipf(2.0) law, so the rarest modes carry single-digit sample counts while the commonest carry thousands — frequencies spanning orders of magnitude. Mode labels are used only for evaluation; no detector sees them (implicit).

Two fault families are injected for the standard benchmark: OOD faults far outside every mode, and pocket faults at midpoints between two well-sampled modes (the dangerous false-negative case). In addition, the C1 generator (Section 5.3) manufactures physically-valid dependency-violation faults by shuffling channel blocks between windows of different modes: every channel retains a real value, and only the joint cross-channel structure is broken. A detector-independent oracle (k-NN distance to a true-normal reference drawn from the same generative process, in the same standardisation frame) certifies which candidates are genuine anomalies, and a pooled-within-mode Mahalanobis distance measures each fault's distance from the nearest mode in σ units. This construction is deliberately kept non-circular: neither the oracle nor the distance metric uses the detector under test.

General vs. CPS normal data. General AD assumes one or a few roughly balanced, often-explicit modes from a stochastic process. CPS normal data is governed by physics and control, has many modes (massive), strongly imbalanced frequencies, and implicit structure that must be discovered — the regime our benchmark reproduces.

5. Methods

5.1 Foundation: joint latent encoding and mode discovery

The foundation is a variational autoencoder whose latent prior is a Gaussian mixture (VaDE [22]): encoder q(z|x), decoder p(x|z), and K learnable mixture components whose means are the operating modes. Encoding and clustering are learned together, so the representation is shaped to make modes emerge as well-separated clusters. A practical obstacle is cluster collapse: naive joint training merged our 40 modes into ~5 components (measured: clustering NMI 0.38, 5/40 components used), crippling rare-mode representation. We stabilise training with a variance floor, a covariance penalty, KL/β warm-up, and a slower learning rate on the mixture parameters; this restores 38/40 components and NMI 0.70, and is decisive for the results below.

5.2 Whitened residual scoring

The anomaly score sums two negative-log-likelihood terms: a reconstruction term and a latent-density term. For reconstruction we use the whitened (Mahalanobis) residual energy ½ r⊤Σ⁻¹r with a Ledoit-Wolf-shrunk residual covariance [30] rather than a plain sum of squares, so per-channel scale and cross-channel correlation are respected. For the latent term we use the nearest-component density (not the π-weighted mixture): a rare-but-valid mode must not be penalised merely for being rare.

5.3 C1 Physically-guided anomaly generation

C1 manufactures the faults that do not otherwise exist, and the ones that matter: physically-valid boundary cases. Candidates are generated by channel-block shuffling across modes (breaking joint structure while keeping marginals real), filtered by the detector-independent oracle to keep only genuine anomalies, and moved a controlled fraction toward the nearest mode to tune difficulty. This yields a spectrum of faults at known distances, which is precisely what is needed to calibrate thresholds and rank detectors without labeled faults.

5.4 C2 Basin-of-attraction test

C2 attacks the false positive. From a point's latent position we run gradient descent on the mixture-density potential from several perturbed restarts and measure basin agreement: the fraction of restarts converging to the same mode. A rare-but-valid point sits in one stable basin (agreement ≈1); a pocket fault sits on the separatrix between two modes and its restarts split (agreement ≈0.5). Crucially this reads geometry, not the rare component's mis-estimated density, so it rescues rare valid modes the density score would alarm on.

5.5 C3 Cluster-based ensemble

C3 attacks the false negative. Rather than one model that averages across all modes and interpolates between them, we fit one reconstruction expert per discovered mode (input-space PCA on that mode's points) and score a point by the minimum reconstruction error over experts — "does any single mode explain this point?" No expert has ever seen the between-modes region, so a pocket fault is reconstructed poorly by all of them, whereas a single global model interpolates and misses it. An owner-gap statistic (second-smallest minus smallest expert error) adds pocket sensitivity.

6.3 C2 and C3 component demonstrations

C2 separates rare-valid modes from pockets by basin agreement: rare-normal points have mean agreement 0.99 (one stable basin) versus 0.78 for pocket faults (split basin), and adding C2 lowers rare-mode FPR from 0.93 to 0.69 (Table 1). C3 catches the over-interpolation false negative: on pocket faults a single global reconstruction model scores AUROC 0.451 (below random — it reconstructs the between-modes bridge too well), while the per-mode ensemble scores 0.996. This is the mechanism the proposal predicts, demonstrated directly; in the ablation (Table 3) adding C3 lifts overall AUROC from 0.946 to 0.997.

7. Limitations and Honest Scope

  • Synthetic only. All results are on the controlled MIIM benchmark. Real-testbed validation is the immediate next step: WADI and SWaT are access-gated (iTrust request, in progress); SKAB and HAI 20.07/21.03 are downloaded and slated next. The code is dataset-agnostic behind a loader.
  • Whitening is neutral here. On this benchmark the Ledoit-Wolf residual whitening neither helps nor hurts because the synthetic observation noise is isotropic; its expected benefit is on real correlated-channel data and is not yet demonstrated.
  • A local-density baseline is competitive. LOF is not blind to the C1 faults (Table 2); the clean claim is that global and single-reconstruction detectors fail, and our structure-aware model leads, not that all baselines fail.
  • Two calibration protocols. The rare-mode FPR (Table 1) and the TPR@5%FPR (Table 2) use different, individually-valid threshold calibrations and are not directly comparable number-to-number.
  • C1 depends on a decoder as physics prior; its "physical validity" is per-channel in-range plus off-joint-manifold, not a full physics simulation.

The framework's claims are demonstrated at the level of a synthetic proof of concept: the joint model beats standard detectors on MIIM data; C1 produces faults that blind standard detectors; C2 reduces rare-mode false positives; C3 catches over-interpolated pockets a single model misses. Transfer to real CPS data is the work that would turn this into an operational result.
